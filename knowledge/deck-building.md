# Exercise 2: Deck Building

**Purpose:** Turn your pitch narrative and story arc into a slide-by-slide pitch deck.

Exercise 1 built the foundation: substance for every element, positioning, and a narrative structure. Now you assemble that into slides. Each slide earns its place by having a clear narrative, credible proof points, and a reason an investor should care. If a slide cannot pass that test, it gets cut or merged.

---

## The 10-Element Structure

Build your deck in this order. Each element maps to one or two slides depending on how much evidence you have. More evidence means you can go deeper. Less evidence means keep it tight and honest.

### 1. Intro / Hook

**What it does:** Grabs attention in the first 30 seconds. Sets the tone for everything that follows.

**What good looks like:** "Financial institutions spend $18B per year manually doing QA, a process we have fully automated." One sentence. Specific. Makes the audience want to know more.

**What bad looks like:** "Welcome to [Company]. Our mission is to transform the way businesses think about quality assurance." Generic. Forgettable. The audience checks their phone.

**Coaching questions:**
- What is the single most surprising thing about this opportunity?
- Does this work for someone who knows nothing about your industry?
- If an investor heard only this sentence and nothing else, would they take the meeting?

Never open with a logo slide. Never open with a mission statement. Never open with your team's credentials. Open with something that makes the problem feel real and urgent.

### 2. The Problem

**What it does:** Shows the pain is real, quantified, and urgent. Makes the audience feel it before you solve it.

**What good looks like:** "87% of SME procurement teams still use email and spreadsheets, costing an average of 12 hours per week in manual reconciliation." Specific segment, specific metric, specific cost.

**What bad looks like:** "Procurement is broken." Vague. Unquantified. Could mean anything to anyone.

**Coaching questions:**
- Who has this problem? How many of them?
- What does it cost them today in money, time, or risk?
- What is the evidence this is real and not your assumption?

### 3. The Solution

**What it does:** Shows how you change the customer's world. Focuses on outcome, not features.

**What good looks like:** "We reduce compliance processing from 3 weeks to 2 hours." Clear before-and-after. Measurable improvement. The audience understands the value instantly.

**What bad looks like:** "We built an AI-powered compliance platform with 47 features." Feature soup. No sense of impact. The word "AI-powered" adds nothing because everyone says it.

**Coaching questions:**
- What changes in the customer's day-to-day life after they adopt your solution?
- Can you describe the outcome without using any jargon?
- What is the single metric that captures your value?

### 4. Market Opportunity

**What it does:** Shows the opportunity is large enough to be venture-scale and that you have calculated it honestly.

**What good looks like:** "12,000 mid-market manufacturers in DACH spend an average of EUR 45K/year on procurement tools = EUR 540M SAM. We target the 3,200 that currently use legacy ERP modules = EUR 144M SOM." Bottoms-up. Verifiable. Specific customer count times specific spend.

**What bad looks like:** "The global procurement market is $12 trillion." Top-down nonsense from an analyst report. No investor believes you will capture a meaningful share of a $12 trillion market.

**Coaching questions:**
- How did you calculate this? Is it bottoms-up or top-down?
- How many customers can you actually reach in year one?
- What is happening in this market right now that creates your window?

### 5. Product

**What it does:** Shows the thing. This is where you prove you have built something real.

**What good looks like:** A live demo, clear screenshots, or a short video walkthrough. The audience sees the product doing what you claimed it does. If you can demonstrate live, do it. Nothing beats a working product shown in real time.

**What bad looks like:** Architecture diagrams. Roadmap slides. Feature comparison matrices. These tell the audience what you plan to build, not what you have built.

**Coaching questions:**
- Can you show it working right now?
- What is the one thing users react to most?
- What would a 30-second demo look like?

### 6. Business Model

**What it does:** Explains how you make money. Shows the economics can work.

**What good looks like:** "SaaS subscription. EUR 500/month per team. Average contract value EUR 6K/year. Current CAC EUR 1,200 via direct sales. Payback period: 2.4 months." Clear pricing, clear unit economics, clear path to margin.

**What bad looks like:** "We will monetise through a freemium model with premium upsells and potential enterprise licensing." Vague. No numbers. No evidence anyone has paid or would pay.

**Coaching questions:**
- How do you charge, who pays, and how much?
- What are your unit economics, or your best estimate of them?
- Is there a path to improving margins over time?

### 7. Traction / Metrics

**What it does:** Proves momentum. Shows that real people or companies are engaging with your product or committing resources to it.

**What good looks like depends on your stage.** Use the Ladder of Proof and show what you actually have. Do NOT show what you do not have.

- **Pre-revenue:** Customer interviews (with count and key quotes), waitlist numbers with engagement data, pilot results, signed letters of intent, design partners.
- **Revenue:** Monthly or annual growth rate, retention/churn, pipeline value, repeat purchase rate, net revenue retention.

**What bad looks like:** Vanity metrics without context. "10,000 downloads" means nothing without retention data. "500 newsletter subscribers" is not traction. Listing metrics you do not have yet ("projected ARR") destroys credibility.

**Coaching questions:**
- What is your single strongest proof point?
- Is it behavioural (signed, paid, used) or stated (said, liked, interested)?
- What would a sceptical investor say about this evidence?

### 8. Team

**What it does:** Answers "Why this team?" At pre-seed, the team IS the investment. Investors are betting on people, not products.

**What good looks like:** One credential per founder that directly connects to the problem you are solving. Founder-problem fit. "Our CTO spent 8 years building procurement systems at Siemens" tells the investor this team understands the problem from the inside.

**What bad looks like:** A grid of headshots with university logos and employer brands that have no relevance to the problem. "Harvard MBA" is not founder-problem fit unless you are solving a problem in business education.

**Coaching questions:**
- Why is this team the right team for this specific problem?
- What direct experience do you have with this pain?
- What have you built before, and what did you learn from it?

### 9. GTM + Vision

**What it does:** Connects where you are today to where you are going. Shows the investor the scale of the opportunity and your plan to get there.

**What good looks like:** A clear go-to-market motion for the next 12 months, followed by a credible expansion path over 5 to 10 years. "Land with mid-market DACH manufacturers via direct sales, expand to adjacent verticals (automotive, pharma) in Year 3, platform play by Year 5."

**What bad looks like:** A vision slide with no connection to your current stage. "We will be the operating system for all global commerce" from a team of three with no revenue.

**Coaching questions:**
- How do you acquire your first 50 customers?
- What is the second act beyond your initial product?
- Why is this a venture-scale outcome?

### 10. The Ask

**What it does:** Tells the investor exactly what you need and exactly what it buys.

**What good looks like:** "We are raising EUR 200K to reach 50 paying customers in 12 months by hiring one engineer and running a 6-month pilot programme with three design partners." Specific amount. Specific milestones. Specific use of funds. The investor can evaluate whether the plan is credible.

**What bad looks like:** "We are raising money to grow." Or worse, no ask at all. Or the vague classic: "We are looking for strategic partners who share our vision."

**Coaching questions:**
- How much are you raising, and how long does it last?
- What specific milestones does this funding unlock?
- What does the company look like when this money is spent?

## Three Deck Versions

You need three versions of your deck. Each serves a different purpose.

### Teaser Deck (5-10 slides)

Goal: get a meeting. Strip the deck to its strongest elements. No sensitive data, no detailed financials, no proprietary IP. This is the version you attach to a cold email or share on a platform. If someone reads it, they should think "I want to learn more." Not "I have all the information I need."

### Presenting Deck (10-12 slides)

Goal: tell the story live. Minimal text, strong visuals. You are the presenter, the slides are the backdrop. If someone reads this deck without you in the room, they should feel like they are missing half the story. That is by design. The spoken word carries the narrative. The slides carry the evidence.

For demo day, build the presenting deck.

### Reading Deck (15-20 slides)

Goal: stand on its own when forwarded. Self-explanatory. Every claim needs context. Every chart needs a label. Every number needs a source. This is the version an associate sends to a partner with "worth a look." The partner will spend 90 seconds deciding whether to take a meeting. Make those 90 seconds count.

## Deck Review

When analysing an existing deck, evaluate each slide against these seven questions.

### Per-Slide Analysis

1. **Is this investor-focused or customer-focused?** Most founders build customer decks and pitch them to investors. The language, framing, and emphasis are wrong for the audience. A customer deck says "here is what our product does for you." An investor deck says "here is why this is a massive opportunity with a credible team and a path to returns."

2. **What is the key narrative?** Can you say it in one sentence? If the slide does not have a single clear message, it is doing too much.

3. **What proof points support it?** Rate each: Strong / Moderate / Weak / Assumption. Be honest. Founders often think their evidence is stronger than it is.

4. **What is the "so what"?** Why should an investor care about this specific slide? What changes in their mental model after seeing it?

5. **What would a sceptical investor ask about this slide?** Think about the hardest question. If you do not have an answer, that is a gap you need to fill.

6. **What is the weakest claim?** Every slide has one. Find it. Either strengthen it with evidence or remove it before an investor does it for you.

7. **Does this slide earn its place, or could it be cut/merged?** Slides that repeat earlier points, add marginal information, or exist only because "we always include one" should go. Every slide must advance the narrative.

### Red Flags in Existing Decks

Watch for these. Each one signals a deck that needs significant rework.

- **Logo/mission statement as slide 1.** No hook. The audience has no reason to pay attention.
- **"We use AI" as a differentiator.** Everyone does. This is not positioning. What matters is the outcome your AI enables, not the fact that it exists.
- **Top-down market sizing.** "$12 trillion market" from a Gartner report. No investor believes you will capture a meaningful fraction of this. Build bottoms-up from actual customer counts and actual willingness to pay.
- **Feature lists instead of outcomes.** "47 features including AI-powered analytics, real-time dashboards, and automated reporting." Nobody cares about features. They care about what changes in the customer's world.
- **No ask, or vague ask.** "We are looking for strategic partners" is not an ask. It is a signal that you have not thought through what you need.
- **Customer-focused language in an investor deck.** Phrases like "our platform helps you streamline" belong in a sales deck. Investors are not your users. They want to know about the market, the economics, and the returns.
- **Metrics the startup does not actually have.** Projected ARR, "expected" user growth, "potential" partnerships. If you do not have the number yet, do not show it. Show what you do have.

## Forwardable Email Template

Based on OpenVC best practice. This is the email that gets your deck opened. 648 characters total. Sent by the CEO only. Never by an advisor, accelerator, or intern.

```
Subject: [One-line hook, not your company name]

Hi [First name],

[Why them: 1-2 sentences referencing their portfolio, thesis, or a specific
investment they made. This requires 15 minutes of research per email.
Generic emails get deleted.]

[No-BS value proposition: one sentence. What you do, for whom, and the
result. No features. No roadmap.]

Key signals:
- [Team credential with specific relevance]
- [Product status: live, in pilot, revenue]
- [Growth metric with number]
- [Named customer, partner, or investor if available]

[Social proof or commitment if you have it: "Backed by X" or
"Selected for Y programme" or "Closing EUR 200K with EUR 140K committed."]

Would you be open to a 30-minute call next week?

[Your name]
[Title, Company]
```

**Rules for this email:**
- Personal greeting, never "Dear Sir/Madam" or "To whom it may concern."
- The "why them" lead is non-negotiable. 15 minutes of research per email. Reference their portfolio, thesis, a blog post they wrote, or a talk they gave. If you cannot find a reason to email this specific person, do not email them.
- Value proposition in one sentence. No features, no roadmap, no "we are disrupting."
- Positive signals as bullets. Names and numbers. Not "strong growth" but "3x revenue growth Q1 to Q2."
- Clear CTA. "Would you be open to a 30-minute call next week?" Not "let me know if you would like to learn more."
- Short is good. Investors receive hundreds of emails. Respect their time.

## Visual Design Principles

These apply to all three deck versions.

- **One idea per slide.** If you need a second idea, you need a second slide.
- **Minimal text on presenting decks.** The slides support what you say. They do not replace you. If you can read your slide verbatim as a script, there is too much text on it.
- **Large fonts.** 30pt minimum for body text. If the person in the back row cannot read it, the font is too small.
- **Consistent colour palette.** Pick two or three colours and stick with them. Your brand colour for emphasis, dark for text, light for backgrounds.
- **Charts and visuals over tables.** A line chart showing growth is processed in 2 seconds. A table of 12 monthly revenue figures takes 30 seconds and loses the audience.
- **Leave white space.** Crowded slides feel desperate. Clean slides feel confident. When in doubt, remove something.
- **No clip art, no stock photos of handshakes, no word clouds.** These signal that the founder prioritised decoration over substance.
- **Consistent alignment and spacing.** Misaligned elements make the whole deck feel unprofessional. Use your slide tool's alignment guides.
