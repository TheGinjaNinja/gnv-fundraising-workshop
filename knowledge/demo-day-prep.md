# Exercise 4: Demo Day Prep

**Purpose:** Polish your pitch until it is sharp, timed, and confident.

You have the evidence (Exercise 1), the deck (Exercise 2), and an honest view of your readiness (Exercise 3). Now make the delivery excellent. A great story told badly is a wasted opportunity. This exercise is about repetition, refinement, and ruthless editing until every second of your pitch earns its place.

---

## Timed Run-Throughs

Every pitch has a time limit. Respect it. Going over time signals poor preparation and disrespect for the audience.

### 3-Minute Version (Tight Demo Day)
- This is the standard demo day format. Every second counts.
- You have roughly 20-25 seconds per slide for a 10-slide deck. That is brutally tight.
- Cut anything that is not essential. If a slide does not move the story forward, remove it.
- Practise with a visible timer. Stop when time is up, even mid-sentence.

### 5-Minute Version (Extended Format)
- Room for a live demo or a detailed customer story.
- Still tight. Do not treat extra time as permission to ramble.
- Use the extra time for the strongest piece of evidence you have, whether that is a product demo, a customer quote, or a traction chart.

### The Drill
- Present the full pitch. Get feedback. Revise. Present again.
- Minimum two full run-throughs per session. Five or more before demo day.
- Record yourself if possible. Watching yourself pitch is painful but invaluable.
- Time every run-through. Track whether you are getting tighter or looser.

## TG vs TTI Diagnostic

Developed by Jason Yeh. A simple but powerful way to measure whether your pitch hooks people.

- **TG** = Time Given. How long is your pitch slot?
- **TTI** = Time to Interest. How many seconds until the audience genuinely engages?
- The gap between TG and TTI is your working time. A large gap means you are wasting your opening.

### How to Measure
- Have someone watch your pitch and note the moment they first feel genuinely interested. Not politely attentive. Actually interested.
- If engagement comes after 30+ seconds, your hook is failing. You are spending too long on setup, introductions, or context.
- Track TTI across run-throughs. The gap should shrink with each iteration.

### The Goal
- Hook the audience in the first 10-15 seconds.
- By the time you reach your second slide, they should already want to hear more.
- If your TTI is longer than 20 seconds in a 3-minute pitch, you are giving away a third of your time before anyone cares.

## Hook Refinement Drill

Your opening line is the most important sentence in the pitch. Test multiple approaches and pick the one that lands hardest.

### Approaches to Try
- **Startling statistic:** "87% of procurement decisions in emerging markets are still made by email and spreadsheet."
- **Provocative question:** "What if you could cut your customer acquisition cost by 80% overnight?"
- **Vivid customer pain story:** "Last month, a hospital in Pristina lost three days chasing a single purchase order through seven departments."
- **Bold claim:** "We have built the fastest way to validate product-market fit, and we can prove it."
- **Named shift:** "The way companies buy software changed permanently in 2024, and most vendors have not noticed."

### Common Failures
These openings kill momentum. Avoid them.
- "Hi, I am [name] and we are [company]." Nobody cares who you are yet. Make them care first.
- "Our mission is to..." Mission statements are not hooks. They are corporate wallpaper.
- "Let me tell you about our journey." Your journey is not interesting until the audience cares about the destination.
- "Thank you for having me." Polite, but it wastes your most valuable seconds.
- "So, um, basically what we do is..." Confidence starts with the first word.

### The Test
- Try each hook approach with a different person. Note which one makes them lean forward.
- The right hook creates a question in the listener's mind that they need answered. It opens a loop.
- Once you find the hook that works, memorise it word for word. Your opening line should never be improvised.

## Q&A Preparation

Investors will ask hard questions. Prepare for the hardest ones so you answer with confidence instead of panic.

### The 5 Hardest Questions
Prepare a 30-second answer for each of these. Practise until you can deliver them without hesitation.

1. **"What evidence do you have that anyone will pay?"** Lead with the strongest behavioural evidence you have. Signed contracts, completed transactions, paid pilots. If you have none, say what you are doing to get it.

2. **"How is this different from [competitor]?"** Do not dismiss the competitor. Acknowledge them, then explain your specific advantage. Use Dunford's positioning framework: competitive alternative, differentiated value, who cares most.

3. **"Why is this team the right team?"** Connect your background directly to the problem. Domain expertise, relevant experience, founder-problem fit. If you lack something, say who you plan to hire and why.

4. **"What happens if [big company] builds this?"** Never say "they are too slow" or "they do not care." Instead, explain why your approach is structurally different. Speed, focus, customer intimacy, technical architecture.

5. **"How do you plan to acquire customers?"** Be specific. Name the channels, the cost per acquisition, the conversion funnel. "We will do digital marketing" is not an answer. "We are acquiring customers through LinkedIn outreach at EUR 45 per qualified lead" is.

### Handling the Unknown
- For questions you genuinely cannot answer: "I do not know yet, but here is how we plan to find out." This is honest and shows process thinking.
- Never argue with the questioner. Even if they are wrong, a debate makes you look defensive.
- Never bluff. Investors spot it instantly and it destroys trust.
- If a question reveals a genuine gap, acknowledge it. "That is a great question and honestly a gap we need to address. Here is our plan."

## Common Demo Day Mistakes

Ten mistakes that sabotage otherwise good pitches. Avoid every one.

1. **Reading slides.** Your slides are for the audience. You should know the content cold. If you need to read, you have not practised enough.
2. **Apologising or hedging.** "We are just a small team" or "This is early stage, so..." Investors know the stage. Do not give them reasons to doubt you.
3. **Logo as slide 1.** Your logo means nothing to an investor. Start with the hook. They will learn your name when they care about your company.
4. **Running over time.** This is the single most common mistake and the easiest to avoid. Practise with a timer. Every time.
5. **Weak close.** Ending with "any questions?" or trailing off. Your last line should be as strong as your first. State the ask clearly and end with energy.
6. **Feature dumping.** Listing features instead of telling a story. Investors buy outcomes, not feature lists. One compelling use case beats ten bullet points.
7. **Showing metrics you do not have.** Do not put a TAM slide up with numbers you cannot defend. If your metrics are early, be honest about what they are and what they mean.
8. **Turning Q&A into a debate.** Answer the question, thank the questioner, move on. You are not there to win an argument. You are there to build trust.
9. **Speaking too fast.** Nerves make everyone speed up. Consciously slow down. Pauses are not awkward. They are powerful.
10. **Not practising enough.** Five full run-throughs is the minimum. Ten is better. The best demo day pitches look effortless because the founder has rehearsed until the content is automatic and they can focus entirely on delivery.

## Delivery Coaching

How you say it matters as much as what you say. These are the physical and vocal elements that separate a good pitch from a great one.

- **Pace:** Slower than feels natural. When you are nervous, your internal clock speeds up. What feels like a normal pace to you sounds rushed to the audience.
- **Pauses:** Use them deliberately after key claims. A pause after a strong number or a bold statement lets it land. Silence is not dead air. It is emphasis.
- **Eye contact:** Work the room. Do not stare at one person or at the back wall. Connect with individuals for 2-3 seconds each. In a large room, look at different sections.
- **Stance:** Plant your feet. Do not pace, sway, or shift weight. Stillness conveys confidence. Movement conveys anxiety.
- **Hands:** Gesture naturally to emphasise points. Do not grip the podium, cross your arms, or put your hands in your pockets. If you do not know what to do with your hands, hold them loosely at your sides.
- **Voice:** Vary pitch and volume. A monotone voice puts people to sleep regardless of content. Get louder for key claims. Get quieter for important moments. Range creates engagement.
- **Confidence:** This is not a personality trait. It is a result of preparation. If you have practised enough, you will feel confident. If you do not feel confident, practise more.
- **Energy:** Match the room and raise it slightly. If the audience is low energy, be warmer and more engaging. If the room is buzzing, match that intensity. Never be the least energetic person in the room.

## Day-of Checklist

Seven things to have sorted before you walk on stage. No exceptions.

1. **Deck loaded and tested on the actual hardware.** Fonts render differently on different machines. Colours shift on different projectors. Test it on the day, on the equipment you will use.
2. **Backup on USB and emailed to yourself.** Technology fails. Have a plan B. And a plan C.
3. **Water nearby.** Dry mouth is real. Take a sip before you start. Have water within reach.
4. **Timer visible.** Whether it is a phone, a watch, or a countdown on the stage monitor, you need to know where you are on time without guessing.
5. **First line memorised (your hook).** Do not improvise your opening. The first ten seconds set the tone for everything. Know it word for word.
6. **Last line memorised (your ask).** Do not trail off. Your close should be as sharp and deliberate as your open. State the ask, deliver the final line, stop talking.
7. **Five Q&A answers rehearsed.** You have prepared for the hardest questions. Run through them once more backstage. Confidence in Q&A comes from knowing your answers before the questions are asked.
