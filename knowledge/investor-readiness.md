# Exercise 3: Investor Readiness

**Purpose:** Honestly assess where you stand and understand what comes after demo day.

Demo day is not the finish line. It is one event within a much longer fundraising process. This exercise helps founders take an honest look at their readiness across the dimensions that investors actually evaluate, understand the signals that matter, and plan what comes next.

---

## OpenVC 7+1 Self-Assessment

Score yourself 1-5 on each dimension. Be honest. A low score is not a problem if you know about it. A low score you have not noticed will kill your raise.

### 1. Problem (Score: /5)
- Is the problem real and validated, or your assumption?
- Can you quantify it? How much does it cost the people who have it?
- How urgent is it? Is this a painkiller or a vitamin?
- How many people have told you about this problem unprompted, without you leading them to it?

### 2. Solution (Score: /5)
- Does it actually work today? What is the evidence?
- Have real users tried it? Did they come back without being prompted?
- Can you demonstrate it live without anything breaking?
- Is it solving the problem you set out to solve, or a different one?

### 3. Business Model (Score: /5)
- How do you make money? Can you explain it in one sentence?
- What are your unit economics, even if estimated?
- Is there a path to healthy margins, or does every sale cost more than it earns?
- Have you tested pricing with real customers?

### 4. Competitive Advantage (Score: /5)
- What do you have that is genuinely defensible?
- Could a well-funded competitor copy this in six months?
- Is your advantage technical, data-driven, network-based, or brand-based?
- Are you honest about your real competitors, including "doing nothing"?

### 5. Scalability (Score: /5)
- Can you grow revenue without a linear increase in costs?
- What breaks first as you scale? Hiring, infrastructure, operations?
- Is there a repeatable sales motion, or does every deal require bespoke effort?
- Can your product serve 10x more customers without 10x more work?

### 6. Capital Efficiency (Score: /5)
- What is your monthly burn rate relative to progress?
- How much runway do you have?
- What have you achieved per euro spent so far?
- Could you have achieved more with the same capital?

### 7. Unit Economics (Score: /5)
- What does it cost to acquire a customer (CAC)?
- What is the lifetime value of a customer (LTV)?
- What is the payback period?
- Even rough estimates count. "We don't know" is a red flag at any stage.

### +1: Team (Score: /5)
- What relevant track record does the team have?
- Do founders have deep domain expertise in the problem space?
- Are skills complementary or overlapping?
- Is there genuine founder-problem fit? Why is this team the one to solve this?

### Scoring Guide

- **Below 20:** Significant gaps to address before raising. Focus on validation and evidence-gathering.
- **20-30:** Fundable, but expect a hard negotiation. Know your weak spots and have a plan to address them.
- **30+:** Strong position. Focus on finding the right investors and telling a compelling story.

Your score is a diagnostic tool, not a verdict. Every startup has gaps at early stage. The question is whether you know where they are and have a credible plan to close them.

## Signal Assessment

Not all traction is equal. Investors pattern-match on signals. Know which tier yours fall into.

### Weak Signals (Do Not Try to Raise on These Alone)
- "Great team" with no relevant domain experience
- "In talks" with a fund but no commitment
- Mockup, MVP, or waitlist with no engagement data
- Patent pending without product-market validation
- "I am an inventor" without commercial traction
- These signals might get you a meeting. They will not close a round.

### Decent Signals (Potential, But Expect Hard Negotiation)
- Ex-FAANG or notable corporate team with relevant skills
- Soft commit from a known fund or angel
- 1,000+ active users with engagement metrics
- Pilot running with a corporate customer
- Patent granted with clear commercial application
- These signals show momentum. Investors will dig deeper and negotiate harder.

### Strong Signals (Go for It)
- Founders with two or more successful exits
- Known fund leading the round with a term sheet
- 30%+ month-on-month MRR growth sustained over three or more months
- Signed contracts with corporate customers generating real revenue
- These signals create investor FOMO. Use them to drive calendar density and competition.

## Novelty vs Traction Matrix

Where your startup sits on the novelty spectrum changes what investors need to see.

**High novelty** (new category, breakthrough technology, paradigm shift): investors buy the vision and the team. Less traction is needed, but the story must be crystal clear and the team must be deeply credible. Expect longer conversations and more education of investors.

**Low novelty** (better mousetrap, existing category, clear competitors): investors buy the numbers. More traction is needed because the opportunity is easier to understand and easier to benchmark. Your metrics need to stand up against known comparables.

**Know where you sit.** If you are creating a new category, do not apologise for limited traction. Lean into the vision. If you are in an existing category, do not waste time explaining why the market is big. Show that you are winning in it.

## Regional Investor Landscape

Every geography has a different investor ecosystem. Before approaching anyone, understand who invests at your stage, in your sector, in your region.

### Investor Categories
- **Angel investors:** individuals writing personal cheques, often with sector expertise
- **Angel syndicates:** groups of angels pooling capital behind a lead investor
- **Early-stage VCs:** institutional funds focused on pre-seed through Series A
- **Grants and public funding:** non-dilutive capital from government or EU programmes
- **Accelerators:** structured programmes combining capital, mentorship, and network
- **Diaspora networks:** investors from your region who are based internationally
- **Corporate venture arms:** strategic investors from large companies in your sector
- **Family offices:** private wealth vehicles with flexible mandates and longer time horizons

### How to Identify the Right Investors
- Filter by stage (pre-seed, seed, Series A). Most investors have a sweet spot.
- Filter by sector. A fintech VC will not lead a biotech round.
- Filter by geography. Some funds invest only locally. Others are regional or global.
- Check their recent portfolio. Have they invested in something similar? That is either a conflict or a fit.
- Look at ticket size. A fund that writes EUR 2M cheques is not leading your EUR 200K round.

### Kosovo and Western Balkans Specifics
- **EU grants:** Horizon Europe, Western Balkans Enterprise Fund, IPA (Instrument for Pre-Accession Assistance)
- **Diaspora angel networks:** significant untapped capital from Kosovar and Albanian diaspora in Switzerland, Germany, Nordics, UK, and US
- **Regional VCs:** South Central Ventures (Balkans-focused), Eleven Ventures (SEE), Credo Ventures (CEE)
- **International accelerator capital:** Swiss EP, Techstars, Plug & Play, and similar programmes that accept Balkan startups
- **Swiss/DACH connections:** strong cultural and economic ties, particularly through the diaspora. Swiss investors increasingly active in the region.

## Investor Types

| Type | Typical Ticket | Decision Speed | What They Want |
|------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| Angel Investors | EUR 5-50K | Fast (days to weeks) | Personal conviction, founder relationship |
| Angel Syndicates | EUR 50-250K | Moderate (2-6 weeks) | Lead investor thesis, social proof |
| Early-stage VC | EUR 250K-2M | Slow (4-12 weeks) | Fund-returner potential, large market |
| Corporate VC | EUR 250K-5M | Very slow (8-16 weeks) | Strategic alignment, market intelligence |
| Family Offices | EUR 100K-5M+ | Variable | Diversification, personal interest |

Match your raise to the investor type. A EUR 300K pre-seed round is an angel and syndicate game. A EUR 2M seed round needs VC involvement. Mixing investor types in a single round is common but requires managing different expectations and timelines.

## Commitment Ladder

Investor interest is not binary. It is a ladder. Know which rung each investor is on.

1. **Feedback (low commitment):** They critiqued your deck or gave advice. This is not investment interest. It is politeness.
2. **Intro offer (moderate):** They offered to connect you to someone in their network. This shows they think you are worth someone else's time.
3. **Soft commit (high):** They said they would invest if others do. This is real but conditional. You need a lead to unlock it.
4. **Term sheet (committed):** Close it. Move fast. Do not renegotiate unless something material has changed.

Track every investor interaction and know where each one sits on this ladder. Most founders overestimate where their investors are. A coffee meeting is not a soft commit. An email saying "keep me posted" is not an intro offer.

## Investor Psychology

Understand what drives investment decisions at the emotional level.

**Greed:** "I want to find the next unicorn." This is what gets you the meeting. Investors take meetings because they are afraid of missing something big. Your pitch should trigger this instinct.

**Fear:** "I do not want an embarrassing failure." This is what makes them say no. Every investor has a mental checklist of things that could go wrong. Your pitch needs to address the obvious risks before they become objections.

**Trust is the currency.** Early-stage investing is fundamentally a bet on people. Trust is built through consistency (you do what you say), specificity (you know your numbers cold), and credibility (your background makes this believable).

**What are early-stage investors actually buying? You.** At pre-seed and seed, the product will change, the market understanding will evolve, the business model will pivot. The one constant is the founding team. Investors are betting that you will figure it out, adapt, and execute. Make sure your pitch gives them confidence in that bet.

## 4-Phase Fundraising Process

Demo day sits within a broader fundraising process. Understand the full picture.

1. **Prepare (50% of effort):** Build the deck, refine the narrative, gather evidence, create the data room, build the target investor list. This is where most of the work happens. Demo day falls within this phase.
2. **Build Network (20%):** Get warm introductions, attend events, build relationships with investors before you need their money. Start this months before you plan to raise.
3. **Calendar Density (20%):** Compress your investor meetings into the tightest possible window. Momentum creates urgency. Urgency creates competition. Competition improves terms.
4. **Close (10%):** Convert soft commits to term sheets. Negotiate terms. Complete due diligence. Sign documents.

Demo day is one event within Phase 1. It is valuable for exposure and practice, but it is not a fundraising strategy on its own. The assessment in this exercise shows you what comes next.

## What to Do After Demo Day

The 48 hours after demo day matter more than the event itself.

- **Follow up within 24 hours** with every investor who expressed interest. Speed signals seriousness.
- **Send the reading deck** (not the presenting deck) as your follow-up. The reading deck stands alone with full detail. The presenting deck needs you in the room.
- **Track every interaction in a CRM.** Use a spreadsheet if nothing else. Record who you met, what they said, what the next step is, and when you will follow up.
- **Start building your investor network now**, even if you are not raising yet. The best fundraises happen when investors already know you before you ask for money.
- **Use the forwardable email template** from Exercise 2. Make it easy for warm contacts to introduce you. A one-paragraph email with your deck attached is infinitely more useful than "you should meet my friend who is building something cool."
- **Book follow-up meetings within the week.** Investor attention fades fast. If someone said "let's talk more," get a date in the calendar before the momentum disappears.
