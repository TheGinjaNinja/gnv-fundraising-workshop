# Exercise 1: Pitch Narrative

**Purpose:** Build the substance and story for your pitch. Find the narrative that makes investors lean in.

This is the most important exercise in the workshop. Most founders start with slides. You start with the story. The pitch narrative comes first because every other deliverable (the deck, the one-pager, the data room) draws from it.

---

## Phase 1: Pitch Substance

For each of the 10 elements in your pitch, establish three things:

- **Key Narrative:** The one thing this slide says. If the audience remembers nothing else from this slide, what is it?
- **Proof Points:** Numbers, facts, quotes, data. What backs up the narrative and makes it credible?
- **So What?:** The impact. Why should an investor care? What changes because of this?

### Template

| Slide | Key Narrative | Proof Points | So What? |
|-------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| Hook  |              |             |          |
| Problem |            |             |          |
| Solution |           |             |          |
| Market |             |             |          |
| Product |            |             |          |
| Business Model |     |             |          |
| Traction |           |             |          |
| Team |               |             |          |
| Vision |             |             |          |
| The Ask |            |             |          |

Fill every cell. If a cell is empty, that is a gap in your pitch. Better to find it now than on stage.

### Coaching Questions

Use these to stress-test each element. Push the founder to go deeper on anything that feels thin.

**Hook:**
- What is the single most surprising thing about this opportunity?
- If you had 10 seconds and one sentence, what would make someone lean in?
- Does your hook work for someone who knows nothing about your industry?

**Problem:**
- Who has this problem? How many of them?
- How do they solve it today? What does it cost them (money, time, reputation)?
- What is the evidence this is real and not your assumption?

**Solution:**
- How does your solution change the customer's life? What do they stop doing, start doing, or do differently?
- Can you explain this without jargon to someone outside your industry?
- What is the core insight that makes your approach work?

**Market:**
- How did you calculate your market size? Is it top-down or bottoms-up?
- What is your serviceable obtainable market in year one?
- What is happening in this market right now that creates your opportunity?

**Product:**
- What does the product actually do? Can you show it?
- What is the one feature that customers value most?
- How is this different from what exists today?

**Business Model:**
- How do you make money? One sentence.
- What are your unit economics (or projected unit economics)?
- Is there a path to improving margins over time?

**Traction:**
- What is your strongest proof point? Is it behavioural (signed, paid, used) or stated (said, liked, interested)?
- What would a sceptical investor say about this evidence?
- What milestone comes next, and when?

**Team:**
- Why is this team the right team for this specific problem?
- What direct experience do you have with this problem?
- What have you built before?

**Vision:**
- Where does this company go in 5 to 10 years?
- What is the second and third act beyond your initial product?
- Why is this a venture-scale outcome?

**The Ask:**
- How much are you raising?
- What specific milestones will it fund?
- How long does this give you, and what happens at the end of that runway?

### Pitch Readiness

Rate every proof point honestly. Investors will probe the weak ones.

- **Strong:** backed by real data you can cite on stage. Revenue, customers, contracts, measured outcomes, direct quotes.
- **Moderate:** supported but not bulletproof. Pilot results, letters of intent, expert benchmarks. Good enough to present confidently, but expect follow-up questions.
- **Weak:** based on secondary research, analogies, or estimates. Market reports, "we believe" statements. Prepare a confident answer for when investors challenge these.
- **Assumption:** your hypothesis. Totally fine at pre-seed. Own it honestly rather than pretending it is proven.

Know which parts of your pitch are strongest and where investors will push back. This helps you prepare, not paralyse you.

## Phase 2: Positioning Diagnostic

How you position your startup determines the shape of your entire pitch. Two frameworks help you find your angle.

### Sequoia PMF Archetypes

These describe the nature of the problem you are solving, which shapes how you open and how much education the audience needs.

- **Hair on Fire:** The problem is urgent. Buyers are actively looking for solutions. Lead with problem severity and speed to value. Your pitch can move fast because the audience already feels the pain.
- **Hard Fact:** The problem is known but normalised. People live with it because they assume it cannot be fixed. You need to reframe how the audience thinks about the problem. Show them what they have been accepting unnecessarily.
- **Future Vision:** The problem is not widely recognised yet. You are building for a world that does not fully exist. You need to educate the audience on the shift that is coming and why it matters.

### April Dunford's Positioning Styles

These describe your competitive stance, which shapes your market slide and how you talk about alternatives.

- **Head-to-Head:** Compete directly with an established player. You need clear, measurable advantages. Lead with differentiation.
- **Dominate a Subsegment:** Own a niche that incumbents underserve. Show deep understanding of a specific customer and why generalist tools fail them.
- **Category Creation:** Define a new space. This is the hardest positioning to pull off but the most powerful when it works. You need to name the category and explain why it exists now.

### The Market Framing Question

Ask yourself: "What is happening in this market that creates my opportunity?"

The answer might be a regulatory shift, a technology inflection, changing customer behaviour, or a demographic trend. This framing question connects your startup to something bigger than your product.

### A Note on Fit

These frameworks are starting points, not boxes. Many startups have positioning that does not map cleanly to a single archetype. Your company might combine a Hair on Fire problem with a Category Creation positioning. Or you might have a geographic advantage, a pricing disruption, a consolidation play, or a community-driven model that sits outside these categories. Use the frameworks to sharpen your thinking, then describe your positioning in your own words.

## Phase 3: Story Arc Selection

Choose the narrative structure that fits your startup's stage, positioning, and evidence base. You do not need to use all three. Pick one and commit.

### Framework 1: Demo Day Arc (Default)

This is the standard investor pitch structure. It works for most startups at most stages. If you are unsure which to use, use this one.

**1. Hook (30 seconds max)**
A startling stat, a provocative question, or a vivid pain story. If your hook does not make someone look up from their phone, it is not a hook. Do not open with your company name, your team's credentials, or "we are building a platform." Open with something that makes the problem feel real and urgent.

**2. Problem**
Quantified, specific, urgent. This is not a feature description. It is a customer pain story. Name a real person or company experiencing this pain. Make the audience feel it before you solve it.

**3. Solution**
How you solve it, not what the product does. Focus on outcome. "We reduce procurement cycle time from 6 weeks to 3 days" is better than "we built an AI-powered procurement platform."

**4. Traction or Evidence**
Present the strongest proof you have, appropriate to your stage. Pre-seed founders show customer discovery evidence and pilot commitments. Seed founders show revenue or engaged users. Do not apologise for early-stage traction. Present it with confidence and context.

**5. Market Size**
Bottoms-up TAM/SAM/SOM. Start with the number of potential customers you can actually reach, multiply by what they would pay, and build up. Top-down numbers from analyst reports are background context, not your market size.

**6. Business Model**
One line. How you charge, who pays, and why the model scales.

**7. Team**
One credential each. Focus on founder-problem fit. Why does this team understand this problem better than anyone else?

**8. The Ask**
Specific amount, use of funds broken into 2 to 3 categories, and the milestones this funding unlocks. End with what the company looks like when this money is spent.

### Framework 2: Strategic Narrative (Andy Raskin)

Source: Andy Raskin, "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen"

This structure works when you are riding a macro shift, creating a new category, or using Future Vision positioning. It reframes the entire conversation around a change in the world rather than your product.

**1. Name a big, relevant change in the world**
Do not start with your company or product. Start with something undeniable that is happening in the market, industry, or society. This change should be large enough that ignoring it has consequences.

**2. Show there will be winners and losers**
This change rewards those who adapt and punishes those who do not. Create stakes. The audience should feel that standing still is not a safe option.

**3. Tease the promised land**
Paint the future state. Make it desirable but difficult to reach without help. This is not your product. It is the outcome your product enables. The audience should want to live in this future.

**4. Introduce your magic gifts**
Your capabilities are tools that help the hero (your customer) reach the promised land. Frame features as gifts, not technical specifications. Each one should clearly connect to the promised land you described.

**5. Present evidence you can deliver**
Customer success stories, pilot results, or proof points that show you are not just describing a vision but delivering on it. This is where traction meets narrative.

When to use this: your startup is defined by a macro trend (AI, climate, regulation, demographic shift). Your positioning is Future Vision or Category Creation. You need the audience to understand the world differently before they can understand your product.

### Framework 3: Market Framing Narrative (April Dunford, adapted for investors)

Source: April Dunford, Sales Pitch (2023), adapted for investor audience.

This structure works in crowded markets where positioning is your edge. It helps the audience see the competitive landscape through your eyes before you present your solution.

**1. Share a market insight**
The problem behind the problem that most people do not see. This is your unique perspective on why the market works the way it does and why current solutions fall short in a way that is not obvious.

**2. Survey the alternatives and their trade-offs**
Walk through the competitive landscape through the buyer's eyes. Show what each alternative does well and where it fails. Be fair. Credibility comes from honest assessment, not from dismissing competitors.

**3. Describe the perfect world**
What would the ideal solution look like? Build the criteria for evaluation before you introduce your product. Let the audience arrive at the conclusion that something better should exist.

**4. Introduce your solution and how it fits**
Now bring in your product. Show how it maps to the criteria you just established. The audience should feel like your product is the logical answer to the problem you framed.

**5. Show differentiated value**
Features that are both unique AND valuable. Unique but not valuable is a gimmick. Valuable but not unique is a commodity. You need both.

**6. Present proof**
Evidence that your differentiated value delivers real results. Customer outcomes, performance data, case studies.

**7. The ask**
Same as any pitch. Amount, use of funds, milestones.

When to use this: your market has established competitors and the audience might assume your space is already served. Your positioning is Head-to-Head or Dominate a Subsegment. Your edge is how you think about the problem, not just what you built.

## Supporting Tools

These are techniques and concepts to layer into whichever narrative framework you choose.

**Pitch Length Ladder**
Build your pitch at multiple lengths. Each one forces you to find the essential message.
- 1-sentence: what you do, for whom, why it matters.
- 30-second: add the problem and the solution.
- 60-second: add market opportunity and team.
- 3-minute: full narrative with evidence at each step.
- 5-minute: deep version with a customer story or live demo.

**Novelty vs Traction Matrix**
More novel means less traction is needed to convince, but the idea is harder to explain. Less novel means more traction is needed, but the concept is easier to understand. Know where you sit on this spectrum and adjust your pitch accordingly. If your idea is highly novel, spend more time on education and vision. If it is less novel, lead with numbers.

**Purity of Motivation (Jason Yeh)**
Investors test whether your reason for building is genuine. Founders with lived experience of the problem survive adversity because they are solving something personal. Founders motivated primarily by market research tend to pivot at the first sign of difficulty. Be honest about your motivation and let it come through in how you talk about the problem.

**Say-Do Gap**
Only behavioural evidence counts. Signed agreements, completed transactions, money committed. Verbal enthusiasm is not evidence. When someone says "I would definitely use that," the follow-up question is "Great, shall we start the pilot this week?" Their answer reveals the truth.

**TG vs TTI Diagnostic (Jason Yeh)**
Time Given minus Time to Interest. After each practice pitch, note when the audience first genuinely engaged. Was it second 5 or second 45? Your goal is to hook within the first 10 to 15 seconds. If engagement comes late, restructure your opening.

**Narrative Specificity (Jason Yeh)**
Develop a precise, specific story. Some investors will say "not for me." Others will say "exactly what I have been looking for." Both responses are valuable signals. A hedged pitch that tries not to offend anybody will not excite anybody either.

**Duarte Sparkline (Nancy Duarte)**
Toggle between "what is" (the current painful state) and "what could be" (the future with your solution) throughout your pitch. This rhythm builds and releases tension, keeping the audience engaged. Each toggle should raise the stakes.

**Sam Jones "False Peak"**
Settle the audience on a reasonable vision, then reveal the bigger one. Let them commit to the small idea before you show them the real ambition. The contrast between the expected outcome and the true opportunity creates a moment of surprise that sticks.

## Demo Day Pitch Examples

Four real-world examples that demonstrate different narrative techniques. Study the structure, not just the content.

### 1. Levi Roots, Reggae Reggae Sauce (Dragons' Den, 2007)

Levi opened by singing an original song about his sauce. He broke every convention of a business pitch. But it worked because his brand IS the product. The sauce is Caribbean warmth, personality, and culture. By performing rather than presenting, he let the audience experience what they would be investing in.

Peter Jones and Richard Farleigh invested GBP 50K for 40%. Reggae Reggae Sauce is now stocked in major UK retailers.

**Lesson:** If your product creates an emotional reaction, let the audience feel it first. Do not describe the experience when you can deliver it.

### 2. Aaron Krause, Scrub Daddy (Shark Tank, Season 4, 2012)

Aaron led with his personal backstory. He had built a car buffing pad company and always had dirty hands. He then demonstrated the product live, showing how the sponge texture changes with water temperature. The combination of origin story and live demonstration made the product unforgettable.

Lori Greiner invested $200K for 20%. Over $209M in lifetime sales. Scrub Daddy is the most successful Shark Tank product ever.

**Lesson:** If you can demonstrate it, demonstrate it. An origin story that connects the founder to the problem, combined with a live demo, is one of the most powerful pitch structures.

### 3. Bombas Socks (Shark Tank, Season 6, 2014)

Bombas led with the insight that socks are the most requested item in homeless shelters. They introduced the buy-one-donate-one model before discussing product features. The mission gave the audience a reason to care before the business case gave them a reason to invest.

Daymond John invested $200K for 17.5%. Over $225M in revenue.

**Lesson:** Lead with mission only if the product independently stands on quality. Bombas works because the socks are genuinely better, not just charitable. Mission without product quality is a charity, not a business.

### 4. Sam Jones, Gener8 (Dragons' Den)

Sam used the "False Peak" technique. His first vision was reasonable: "people should have a choice to stop data tracking." The audience nodded. Then he delivered the real vision: "or even better, earn from it themselves." Eight words turned a good pitch into a great one.

Gener8 was valued at over GBP 30M.

**Lesson:** Structure your vision in two parts. Let the audience settle on the expected outcome, then surprise them with the real ambition. The contrast between the reasonable vision and the transformative one creates a moment that sticks in memory.
