Speaker Notes

GNV Fundraising Workshop / Wednesday AM Group Session / Total: ~60 minutes

Slide 1 Title 0:30
Welcome everyone. Quick intro, then straight into it.

Keep this tight. No preamble. Let the room settle, make eye contact, then go.

Slide 2 Who Am I 2:00

Credibility, not CV. Two stories, then move on.

Bondcube. Raised EUR 10M. My co-founder had 15 years in bond trading. That credibility was worth more than any deck we ever built. Investors backed the person, not the slides.
P.S. Bridal. The founder personally experienced the problem. Investors could see she would not quit. That conviction is something you cannot fake.
I have sat where you are sitting. I have also sat on the other side of the table.

Pause after this line. Let it land. Then transition to the reality slide.

Slide 3 Fundraising Reality 3:00
These stats are sobering. But you are here, in an accelerator. That changes everything.
How many of you have pitched to an investor before?

Show of hands. Use this to calibrate the room. If most have pitched before, you can move faster through fundamentals. If most have not, slow down on the evidence and story arc slides.

The 60% success rate through accelerators is the key stat. They are in the right place. Emphasise this. It reframes the scary numbers into something actionable.

Slide 4 What Are Investors Buying? 3:00
WeWork / Adam Neumann. WeWork raised $12B, reached a $47B valuation, then liquidated. Then a16z invested $350M in Adam Neumann's next real estate startup, Flow. Their largest single investment ever. Let that sink in. VCs are not always rational. They are buying the founder.
VCs are not always rational. They are buying the founder. Build trust.
What do you think investors are most afraid of?

Let a few people answer. Common responses: losing money, picking the wrong team, missing a trend. Acknowledge all of them, then use them to bridge into investor psychology on the next slide.

Slide 5 Investor Psychology 2:00

Quick explanation of the greed vs fear dynamic. Do not over-explain. Two emotions, two signals.

Every signal you send either activates catching (excitement about getting in early) or missing (anxiety about being left behind).

Trust is built through three things:

  • Consistency - monthly updates, keeping your word
  • Specificity - exact numbers, precise language
  • Credibility - domain expertise, strong references

Move quickly here. This is context for what comes next, not a deep dive.

Slide 6 Evidence Grid 5:00
Before we touch storytelling, we need substance.

This is the most interactive slide. Spend time here.

Live exercise: ask one team to share their problem statement. Apply the Evidence Grid live on the spot.

What is the evidence for this? What would a sceptical investor say?

Walk through the three columns: key narrative, proof points, the "so what." Show how a vague claim becomes a strong slide when you add real evidence.

If the room is engaged, take a second example from another team. If time is tight, keep to one.

Slide 7 10-Element Deck 5:00

Walk through each element briefly. Do not linger on any single one.

  • Hook / Opening
  • Problem
  • Solution
  • Market
  • Traction
  • Business Model
  • Competition
  • Team
  • The Ask
  • Vision / Close
Most decks fail on slide 1 (no hook) and slide 10 (vague ask). The hook and the ask are the bookends. Everything else is the evidence in between.

Emphasise that this is not a rigid template. The structure holds but the exact slide count per element varies. Some elements are one slide, some are two or three.

Slide 8 Story Arc 5:00

Brief explanation of each framework. Connect each one to the type of evidence a founder has.

The evidence tells you which story to tell.
  • Hair on Fire = Demo Day Arc. The problem is urgent, the evidence is strong, tell it simply.
  • Hard Fact = reframe with Market Framing. The data tells the story.
  • Future Vision = Raskin Strategic Narrative. Name the shift, show winners and losers.
For most of you, the Demo Day Arc is the right choice. It is the simplest and most effective for a 3-5 minute pitch.

Only mention Raskin and Duarte briefly. Most teams will use the Demo Day Arc. Do not overwhelm them with frameworks.

Slide 9 The Hook 3:00

Read the bad hooks out loud. Pause. Let the room react. Then read the good hooks.

Feel the difference?
Your first line is the most important line in your entire pitch. If you lose them in the first 10 seconds, you never get them back.

If time allows, ask one or two founders to share their current opening line. Give quick feedback. This gets the room thinking about their own hooks before the 1:1 sessions.

Slide 10 The Ask 2:00
Specific amount. Specific milestones. Specific timeline.
If you cannot say exactly what the money buys in one sentence, you are not ready to ask for it.

This is a gut-check moment. Keep it punchy. No extended discussion needed. The point should land immediately.

Slide 11 Demo Day Rules 2:00

Quick hits through the list. Read them with energy.

Never start with your logo. Never end without an ask.
If you have not done 5 run-throughs, you have not practised.

Do not elaborate on each rule individually. Let the slide speak. The rules are self-explanatory. Your delivery speed here signals that these are non-negotiable basics, not topics for debate.

Slide 12 What Happens Next 3:00

Repo walkthrough. Open the repo on screen if possible.

  • Show how to clone the repo
  • Show the CLAUDE.md file and explain it is their AI coach
  • Show the FieldFlow worked example so they know what good looks like

Explain the 1:1 schedule for the afternoon. Each team gets a slot.

The AI coach does not sleep. Use it tonight. I am available today and tomorrow morning.

Make sure every team knows how to access the repo before you move on. Ask for a quick thumbs up.

Slide 13 Close 1:00
Go build something people remember.

Hold the silence for a beat. Then open for questions. Keep Q&A to 5 minutes maximum. If a question is specific to one team, defer it to the 1:1 session.
